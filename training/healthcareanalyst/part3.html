3.1 The Sources and Shape of Healthcare Data

Healthcare data lives in distinctive sources an analyst must know. The big one is the EHR/EMR (Electronic Health Record — the digital patient record holding diagnoses, medications, labs, vitals, notes, procedures). Others include claims data (billing/insurance records — rich for cost and utilisation analysis, and often more standardised than clinical data), administrative/operational systems (admissions, scheduling, registries), lab and device data, and public-health datasets. Healthcare data is notoriously messy and complex: it's often fragmented across systems that don't talk to each other, full of missing values and inconsistencies, mixes structured (coded) and unstructured (clinical notes) data, and carries clinical nuance that's easy to misread. Understanding where the data comes from — and that it's typically messier and more complex than data in other fields — sets realistic expectations and is the starting point for working with it effectively.

3.2 Privacy: The Non-Negotiable Duty (Practical)

Health data is among the most sensitive data that exists, and protecting it is a fundamental legal and ethical duty — not an afterthought. This shapes how a healthcare analyst works at every step:

HEALTHCARE DATA PRIVACY (non-negotiable)
  WHY IT'S SPECIAL: health data is among the most sensitive personal data —
    breaches cause real harm; it's heavily regulated by LAW
  THE LAWS (know your region's):
    · HIPAA (US) — protects 'PHI' (Protected Health Information)
    · GDPR (EU) — health data is a 'special category', extra protection
    · equivalents elsewhere — the principles are similar
  CORE PRACTICES:
    · ACCESS CONTROL: only access data you're authorised & need to (minimum necessary)
    · DE-IDENTIFICATION / ANONYMISATION: remove/mask identifiers (names, IDs, dates,
      addresses) before analysis where possible — analyse patterns, not identities
    · SECURE handling: secure systems, no copying to unsafe places, no sharing improperly
    · NEVER use real identifiable patient data for practice/portfolios —
      use SYNTHETIC or properly de-identified/public data
  ETHICS: behind every record is a real person who trusted the system with their data
KEY: privacy is a LEGAL & ETHICAL duty woven through everything you do — not optional.

The privacy duty is woven through everything: know the laws (HIPAA in the US, GDPR in the EU, and regional equivalents — health data is specially protected everywhere), and follow core practices: minimum-necessary access (only access what you're authorised and need), de-identification/anonymisation (analyse patterns, not identities — remove or mask identifiers wherever possible), secure handling, and — critically for your learning — never use real identifiable patient data for practice or portfolios (use synthetic or public data). Behind every record is a real person who trusted the system; treating that data with legal rigour and ethical care is a defining professional responsibility of the role.

3.3 Data Standards and Interoperability

A final piece of healthcare-data literacy: standards and interoperability. Because healthcare data is so fragmented across systems, the industry uses standards to structure and exchange it — the coding standards (ICD, CPT from Part 2) plus data-exchange standards like HL7 and FHIR (modern standards for how health systems share data). An analyst doesn't need to be a standards expert, but understanding that these exist — and that interoperability (getting data from different systems to work together) is a major real-world challenge — is important context. It explains why healthcare data work often involves significant effort just to get and combine the data (much of it lives in incompatible silos), and why standardised sources like claims data are often easier to analyse. Awareness of healthcare data standards and the interoperability challenge rounds out the data literacy that, combined with the domain knowledge (Part 2) and the privacy duty, lets a healthcare analyst work responsibly and effectively with the field's distinctive, sensitive, complex data.

CHECKPOINT

Build a data & privacy primer: the main healthcare data sources (EHR, claims, operational, etc.) and why the data is messy/complex, the privacy duty (the laws like HIPAA/GDPR and the core practices — access control, de-identification, never using real patient data for practice), and an awareness of standards/interoperability (ICD/CPT, HL7/FHIR, the silo challenge). Knowing where health data lives and the non-negotiable duty to protect it is foundational to the role.
