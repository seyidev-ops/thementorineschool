4.1 Mean, Median and the Long Tail

Spend in your data is roughly bell-shaped, but most money data isn't — a few huge values drag the mean far above the median (the middle customer). Rule: report the median for skewed quantities, the mean when you genuinely care about totals, and say which you used. One line tells you how skewed you are: df["monthly_spend"].agg(["mean","median"]) — the wider the gap, the longer the tail.

4.2 Sampling Noise: Why Small Groups Scream (Practical)

FILEpart4-stats/noise.py
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(7)

true_rate = 0.20                      # suppose TRUE churn is exactly 20%
for n in [20, 100, 1000, 10000]:
    samples = [rng.random(n).__lt__(true_rate).mean() for _ in range(1000)]
    lo, hi = np.percentile(samples, [2.5, 97.5])
    print(f"n={n:>5}: measured churn swings between {lo:.0%} and {hi:.0%}")

Run it. With 20 customers, a true 20% churn measures anywhere from 5% to 40% by luck alone; with 10,000 it barely moves. This is the whole intuition behind confidence intervals: small samples scream, big samples whisper — so never rank a 9-customer segment against a 900-customer one without saying so.

4.3 The A/B Test: Did the Change Actually Work? (Practical)

NaijaTel trials a retention offer on a random half of at-risk customers. Group A (control) churns 22%, Group B (offer) churns 18%. Real effect, or luck? The honest answer is a permutation test — shuffle the labels thousands of times and ask how often luck alone produces a gap that big:

FILEpart4-stats/ab_test.py
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(11)

a = rng.random(500) < 0.22          # control: 500 customers
b = rng.random(500) < 0.18          # offer:   500 customers
observed = a.mean() - b.mean()

pooled = np.concatenate([a, b])
gaps = []
for _ in range(10000):
    rng.shuffle(pooled)
    gaps.append(pooled[:500].mean() - pooled[500:].mean())

p_value = (np.abs(gaps) >= abs(observed)).mean()
print(f"observed gap {observed:.1%}, p-value {p_value:.3f}")
print("Verdict:", "likely real" if p_value < 0.05 else "could easily be luck")

The p-value answers exactly one question: if the offer did nothing, how often would chance fake a gap this large? Below 5% we lean “real” — a convention, not a law. Run it a few times with different seeds and watch borderline results flicker; that flicker is why serious teams pre-register sample sizes.

4.4 Correlation Is Not Causation — and Confounders Are Why

In your data, high data-usage customers churn less. Does data usage protect against churn? More likely both are caused by a third thing — engaged customers use more data and stay. That hidden third thing is a confounder, and it is the reason “customers who do X behave like Y” never proves “making customers do X causes Y.” The only clean causal tool you own is the randomised test from 4.3 — randomisation severs confounders by construction. Everything else is a correlation wearing ambition.

GOLDEN RULE

Predictive models only need correlation — they may say “support calls predict churn” freely. The moment anyone says “so reducing support calls will reduce churn,” demand a randomised test. Holding that line is literally part of the job.

4.5 Checkpoint Project: Three Honest Claims

From the clean NaijaTel data, write three claims, each with its honesty equipment: one descriptive (with mean and median), one comparative (two segments, with their sizes stated and a permutation p-value), one predictive-only (a correlation explicitly not dressed as causation, naming a plausible confounder). Three sentences, properly armed, are worth more than any unarmed dashboard.
