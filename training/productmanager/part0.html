0.1 What a Product Manager Actually Does

A product manager (PM) decides what a product should be and why, then leads a team to build the right thing for the right people. They sit at the intersection of business (what's viable and valuable), users (what people actually need), and technology (what's feasible to build) — and their job is to find the overlap: a product that users love, the business benefits from, and the team can build. A PM does this by deeply understanding users and the market, defining a product vision and strategy, deciding what to build and in what order (prioritisation), writing clear requirements, working closely with designers and engineers to ship it, and measuring whether it succeeded — then iterating. Crucially, a PM usually has no direct authority over the team; they lead through vision, influence, and clarity. It's one of the most strategic, influential, well-paid, and remote-friendly roles in tech — often called the 'mini-CEO' of a product.

This guide takes you from zero to job-ready in a recommended 8–12 weeks, using free tools and the real frameworks the profession uses. The capstone, The Product Kit, is a complete set of product-management artefacts for a realistic product — a product vision and strategy, user research and personas, a prioritised backlog, a key feature spec with user stories, a metrics framework, and a roadmap — the exact body of work that proves you can think and work like a product manager and lands the role.

WHY THIS MATTERS

Building the wrong product is the most expensive mistake a company can make — most products fail because nobody needed them. The product manager who can figure out what's worth building, for whom, and why, and lead a team to ship it, is one of the most valued, highly-paid, and influential people in any tech organisation — and the role is remote-friendly and globally in demand.

0.2 The Golden Rules

Fall in love with the problem, not the solution — great PMs obsess over understanding the user's real problem; the solution should serve it, not the other way round. Build what users need, not what's loudest — decisions come from evidence (users, data), not the highest-paid person's opinion or your own assumptions. Say no — a lot — prioritisation is the core of the job; a great product comes from what you don't build as much as what you do. You lead through influence, not authority — you align engineers, designers, and stakeholders by vision and reasoning, not commands. Measure outcomes, not output — success is the impact (users helped, value created), not the number of features shipped.

0.4 Set Up Your Free Toolkit (Practical)

Product management runs on free tools — the value is in the thinking and frameworks. (1) Documents & specs: Google Docs (PRDs, specs, strategy) and Sheets (prioritisation, metrics). (2) Backlog & roadmap: Trello, Notion, or a free tier of Jira/ClickUp for backlogs and boards. (3) Research & design: Google Forms for surveys, and Figma (free) to sketch simple wireframes/flows (you don't design, but you communicate ideas visually). (4) Analytics literacy: understand product metrics (this guide teaches them); tools like GA4 or Mixpanel are common on the job. Set up:

SET UP
1. Google Docs & Sheets + a board tool (Trello/Notion/Jira free) + Figma (free)
2. Make a Google Drive folder 'The Product Kit' with sections:
   Users+Research / Vision+Strategy / Backlog / Specs / Metrics / Roadmap
3. Choose a realistic product to manage throughout — ideally one solving a real problem
   you understand (e.g. 'an app helping Nigerian students find affordable housing',
   or improving a product you use daily)
4. Note: this teaches the universal PM craft & frameworks — transferable to any company/tool

Every lesson: Concept → Hands-On (building real product artefacts) → Try This → per-part Checkpoint. The work compounds into The Product Kit — a complete, professional set of product-management documents for a real-feeling product, which is exactly what proves your product thinking to employers (who hire PMs largely on demonstrated thinking).
