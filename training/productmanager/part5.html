5.1 The PM Defines the What, Not the How

Once a PM has decided what to build (prioritisation), they must communicate it clearly to the people who'll design and build it. A foundational principle: the PM defines the what and why (the problem to solve, the desired outcome, the requirements), while the designers and engineers own the how (the design and technical implementation). The PM shouldn't dictate technical solutions — they should clearly convey the problem, the user need, the goal, and the requirements, then collaborate with the experts on the best solution. Getting this clarity right is essential: vague or constantly-changing requirements waste enormous effort and frustrate teams, while clear, well-thought-out requirements let designers and engineers do their best work. Writing crisp requirements is a core daily PM craft.

5.2 User Stories and Specs (Practical)

Requirements are most commonly captured as user stories (especially in Agile) and, for larger features, a spec/PRD (Product Requirements Document):

REQUIREMENTS: USER STORIES + SPECS
  USER STORY (the standard format): captures a requirement from the USER's view:
    'As a [type of user], I want [goal/action], so that [benefit/reason].'
    e.g. 'As a student, I want to filter listings by price, so that I only see
          places I can afford.'
    -> keeps the focus on the USER & the WHY, not just a feature
  ACCEPTANCE CRITERIA: the conditions that define 'done' for a story
    (what must be true for it to be complete & correct) — removes ambiguity
  A SPEC / PRD (for a larger feature):
    · the PROBLEM & goal (why we're building this)  · the target user
    · the user stories / requirements  · acceptance criteria
    · success metrics (how we'll know it worked)  · scope (what's in/out)
    · (NOT the technical how — leave that to engineering)
  KEEP IT CLEAR & focused on the WHAT/WHY; collaborate on the HOW
GOAL: the team knows exactly what to build, for whom, why, and what 'done' means.

The user story format ('As a [user], I want [goal], so that [benefit]') is the standard because it keeps requirements anchored to the user and the why, not just a feature description. Each story needs acceptance criteria (the clear conditions for 'done') to remove ambiguity. For larger features, a PRD/spec lays out the problem, target user, requirements, acceptance criteria, success metrics, and scope — deliberately leaving the technical 'how' to engineering. Clear requirements like these are what let a cross-functional team build the right thing efficiently.

5.3 Working With Design and Engineering

Defining requirements isn't a hand-off — it's the start of close collaboration with designers and engineers, the people who turn requirements into a real product. The PM works with designers (who shape the user experience and interface — the PM brings the problem and user understanding; the designer brings the design solution) and engineers (who build it — the PM brings the what/why and priorities; engineers bring feasibility, technical insight, and the build). Good PMs involve these experts early (their input improves the product and surfaces constraints), respect their expertise (defining what/why, not dictating how), and maintain a strong collaborative relationship (recall: influence, not authority — the team builds well when they trust and are aligned with the PM). This cross-functional collaboration — bringing business/user clarity to a team of makers and aligning them — is the heart of how a PM actually gets a product built.

CHECKPOINT

Write a feature spec with user stories for a key feature of your product: a short PRD (the problem, target user, user stories in proper format with acceptance criteria, success metrics, and scope), focused on the what/why not the technical how. Note how you'd collaborate with design and engineering. Clear requirements and strong cross-functional collaboration are how a PM communicates and gets the right thing built.
